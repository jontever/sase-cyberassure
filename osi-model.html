How SASE actually enforces — single-pass inspection

The OSI layering in the other tabs is a teaching lens for defence-in-depth reasoning. In a real SASE/SSE cloud PoP, traffic is decrypted once, inspected by all planes in parallel in a single pass, then re-encrypted and forwarded — not passed sequentially down seven layers. This delivers overlapping coverage without the latency of chained appliances.

ZTNA vs PAM — the access boundary

Both are zero-trust access controls, but they answer different questions. ZTNA decides who can reach a resource; PAM decides what a privileged identity can do once connected.

ZTNA — access to applications
  • Identity-aware access — every request authenticated and authorised per session.
  • Least privilege per app — users reach only the specific apps they are entitled to, never the flat network.
  • Continuous verification — device posture and risk re-checked throughout the session.
  • Default-deny — no implicit trust; nothing is reachable until policy explicitly allows it.
  • Broad scope — applies to the whole workforce, for north-south and east-west app access.
  • Shrinks attack surface — applications stay invisible until access is granted.
PAM — privilege within systems
  • Privileged accounts — governs admin, root and service identities, not general users.
  • Credential vaulting — secrets are stored and brokered, never exposed to the operator.
  • Just-in-time elevation — standing privilege is removed; access is granted for a task, then revoked.
  • Session recording — privileged sessions are monitored, recorded and fully auditable.
  • Secret rotation — passwords and keys are rotated automatically after use.
  • Contains blast radius — limits what a trusted insider or stolen admin account can do.

Operating principle: ZTNA is the front door — it brokers entry to the OSI stack at the session layer and shrinks the attack surface. PAM is the inner safe — privileged sessions are vaulted, time-boxed and recorded as they ride the same enforcement stack down to the resource. Together: zero trust for access, least privilege for power. Both stream their decisions up to SecOps, which tunes policy and closes the loop.
